CENSUS OF BOUNDED CUR V ATURE PATHS
JEAN D´IAZ AND JOS ´E AYALA
Abstract. A bounded curvature path is a continuously diﬀerentiable piece-wise C2 path with
bounded absolute curvature connecting two points in the tangent bundle of a surface. These
paths have been widely considered in computer science and engineering since the bound on cur-
vature models the trajectory of the motion of robots under turning circle constraints. Analyzing
global properties of spaces of bounded curvature paths is not a simple matter since the length
variation between length minimizers of arbitrary close endpoints or directions is in many cases
discontinuous. In this note, we develop a simple technology allowing us to partition the space
of spaces of bounded curvature paths into one-parameter families. These families of spaces are
classiﬁed in terms of the type of connected components their elements have (homotopy classes,
isotopy classes, or isolated points) as we vary a parameter deﬁned in the reals. Consequently,
we answer a question raised by Dubins (Pac J Math 11(2):471481, 1961).
1. Prelude
It is well known that any two plane curves both closed or with diﬀerent endpoints are homo-
topic. Graustein, and Whitney in 1937, independently proved that not any two planar closed
curves are regularly homotopic (homotopic through immersions) [31]. Markov in 1857 consid-
ered several optimization problems relating a bound on curvature with the design of railroads
[22]. But, it was only in 1957 that bounded curvature paths were rigorously introduced by
Dubins when bounded curvature paths of minimal length were ﬁrst characterized [15].
Fix two elements in the tangent bundle of the Euclidean plane ( x,X ), (y,Y )∈ T R2. Infor-
mally, a planar bounded curvature path is a C 1 and piecewise C 2 path starting at x, ﬁnishing
at y; with tangent vectors at these points X and Y respectively, having absolute curvature
bounded by κ = 1
r > 0. Here r is the minimum allowed radius of curvature. The piecewise C 2
property comes naturally due to the nature of the length minimizers [15] 1.
In 1961 Dubins raised fundamental questions about the topology of the spaces of bounded
curvature paths [16]. “Here we only begin the exploration, raise some questions that we hope
will prove stimulating, and invite others to discover the proofs of the deﬁnite theorems, proofs
that have eluded us” see pp. 471 in [16]. Fifty years later the fundamental questions proposed
by Dubins were answered through the papers [2, 4, 5, 6]. In addition, the classiﬁcation of
the homotopy classes of curves with bounded absolute curvature, having ﬁxed initial and ﬁnal
positions, and variable initial and ﬁnal directions was achieved in [3].
In this note, we develop an elementary framework enabling us to parametrize families of
spaces of bounded curvature paths. These families share similar types of connected components,
being these: isolated points, homotopy, or isotopy classes, see Theorem 8.5 and Deﬁnition 8.4.
In particular, we answer a question raised by Dubins in 1961 [16] by explicitly describing the
set of endpoints (x,X ), (y,Y )∈T R2 so that the space of bounded curvature paths starting at
x, ﬁnishing at y; with tangent vectors at these points X and Y respectively admits a bounded
isotopy class, see Theorem 8.6 and Corollary 8.7. We conclude by presenting an updated
(parametric) version of the classiﬁcation theorem for homotopy classes of bounded curvature
paths in [6], by incorporating the results here obtained, see Theorem 9.1. Our results can be
extended without much eﬀort for paths in the hyperbolic 2-space.

2. On spaces of bounded curvature paths
For the convenience of the reader, we include relevant material from our previous work in
[2, 3, 4, 5, 6]. Denote by T R2 the tangent bundle of R2. Recall that the elements in T R2 are
pairs (x,X ) denoted here for short by x. The ﬁrst coordinate of such a pair corresponds to a
point in R2 and the second to a tangent vector to R2 at x.
Deﬁnition 2.1. Given (x,X ), (y,Y )∈T R2, a path γ : [0,s ]→ R2 connecting these points is
a bounded curvature path if:
• γ is C 1 and piecewise C 2;
• γ is parametrized by arc length (i.e ||γ′(t)|| = 1 for all t∈ [0,s ]);
• γ(0) =x, γ′(0) =X; γ(s) =y, γ′(s) =Y ;
•|| γ′′(t)||≤ κ, for all t∈ [0,s ] when deﬁned, κ> 0 a constant.
The ﬁrst item means that a bounded curvature path has continuous ﬁrst derivative and
piecewise continuous second derivative. Minimal length elements in spaces of paths satisfying
the last three items in Deﬁnition 2.1 are in fact C 1 and piecewise C 2. For the third item,
without loss of generality, we extend the domain of γ to (−ϵ,s +ϵ) for ϵ > 0. Sometimes
we describe the third item as the endpoint condition. The fourth item means that bounded
curvature paths have absolute curvature bounded above by a positive constant. Without loss
of generality, we consider κ = 1.
The unit tangent bundle UT R2 is equipped with a natural projection p :UT R2→ R2. Note
that p−1(y) is S1 for all y∈ R2. The space of endpoints is a circle bundle over R2.
Remark 2.2. (Coordinate system and angle orientation).
• For the given (x,X ), (y,Y )∈T R2 in Deﬁnition 2.1 we consider a coordinate system so
that the origin is identiﬁed withx, andX with the ﬁrst canonical vector in the standard
basis{X =e1,e 2} for R2.
• When measuring angles we consider the positive orientation to be traveled counterclock-
wise.
Dubins [15] proved that the length minimizer bounded curvature paths are necessarily a
concatenation of an arc of a unit radius circle, followed by a line segment, followed by an arc
of a unit radius circle, the so-called csc paths. Or, a concatenation of three arcs of unit radius
circles, the so-called ccc paths. After considering r be a circle traveled to the right and l a
circle travelled to the left we obtain six possible types of paths, namely lsl, rsr, lsr, rsl,
lrl and rlr. Paths having one of these types are here called Dubins paths. Note that we are
considering Dubins path to be local not necessarily global minimum of length.

A bounded-curvature isotopy is a continuous one-parameter family of embedded bounded
curvature paths. Two paths in Γ( x,y) are bounded-homotopic (bounded-isotopic) if there exists
a bounded curvature homotopy (isotopy) from one to the other. A homotopy (isotopy) class is
a maximal path connected set in Γ( x,y).
Deﬁnition 2.8. Let ∆(x,y) be a non-empty bounded isotopy class of paths in Γ( x,y). Let B
denote the set of pairs x,y∈T R2 for which Γ(x,y) possesses such a bounded isotopy class.
In [5] we proved that B̸=∅ by establishing the existence of non-empty bounded isotopy
classes. Whenever we refer to ∆( x,y) we imply that ∆(x,y) is non-empty. In this note, we give
necessary and suﬃcient conditions so that x, y∈ T R2 is an element in B. As a consequence,
we answer a question raised by Dubins in pp. 480 in [16]. We establish that B is a bounded
neither open nor closed subset in T R2, see Theorem 8.6 and Corollary 8.7.
2.2. Proximity of endpoints. Here we analyze the conﬁgurations of distinguished pairs of
circles in R2. This approach permits us to reduce the conﬁgurations of endpoints in T R2 into
a ﬁnite number of cases up to isometries.
Consider x∈ T R2. Let Cl(x) be the unit radius circle tangent to x and to the left of X.
The meaning of Cr(x), Cl(y) and Cr(y) should be obvious. These circles are called adjacent
circles. Denote the centers of the adjacent circles with lowercase letters. So, the center of Cl(x)
is cl(x), see Fig. 5 right. The other cases are analogous.
We concentrate on the following conﬁgurations for the adjacent circles.
(i) d(cl(x),cl(y))≥ 4 and d(cr(x),cr(y))≥ 4
(ii) d(cl(x),cl(y))< 4 and d(cr(x),cr(y))≥ 4
(iii) d(cl(x),cl(y))≥ 4 and d(cr(x),cr(y))< 4
(iv) d(cl(x),cl(y))< 4 and d(cr(x),cr(y))< 4
The conditions (i)-(iv) have being used in diﬀerent contexts through [2, 4, 5, 6]. They give
information about the topology and geometry of Γ( x,y). Note that as planar conﬁgurations,
(ii) and (iii) are equivalent up to isometries.
2.3. Trapped regions and bounded isotopy classes. In Theorem 5.4 in [6] we proved that
for certain x,y∈T R2, the associated space Γ( x,y) admits a bounded isotopy class ∆( x,y). It
turns out that paths in ∆( x,y) are deﬁned exclusively in a bounded region Ω⊂ R2. The shape
of Ω depends on the initial and ﬁnal positions and directions in T R2, see Fig. 6.
For a precise explanation on how these regions Ω ⊂ R2 are constructed, we strongly suggest
the reader refer to Section 4 in [5]. We call these regions trapped regions.
It is important to note that:
• Embedded paths in Ω cannot be deformed without violating the curvature bound to a
path with a self-intersection, see Corollary 7.13 in [5].
• Embedded paths in Ω are not bounded-homotopic to paths having a point not in Ω, see
Theorem 8.1 in [5].
• The proof of the existence of isolated points in spaces of bounded curvature paths
was given in Theorem 3.9 in [5]. These correspond to arcs of a unit circle of length
less to π, called c isolated points . Similarly, a concatenation of two arcs of unit
circle, each of length less to π, are called cc isolated points . Isolated points in
Γ(x,y) are bounded isotopy classes with empty interior, see Fig. 1 left, and Fig. 7
left. In addition, bounded curvature paths of length zero are also isolated points. This
observation becomes interesting after recalling the concept of simple connectedness.
Closed bounded curvature paths are not bounded-homotopic to a single point.

4. Motivation through examples
In narrative terms, here we present facts in reverse-chronology. By considering this strategy,
we are telling the reader “the end of the story” through various examples, with the intention
to motivate the more technical steps and proofs.
We study the ﬁbers in Γ by ﬁxingx = (x,X )∈T R2 and a ﬁnal positiony∈ R2 while varying
the ﬁnal direction Yθ∈ TyR2, θ∈ R. In Section 7 we construct a function, called the class
range, that assigns to each ﬁnal position y∈ R2 a non-negative real number. This number is
called the class value and gives the range θ can vary so that the spaces Γ( x, yθ) have the same
types of connected components.
Firstly, we would like to point out that for a ﬁxed θ∈ R, the spaces Γ( x, ykθ) and Γ( x, yjθ)
may eventually be diﬀerent for j̸= k∈ Z. Secondly, consider x, yθ∈ T R2, so that θ =±π.
Since the initial and ﬁnal tangent vectors are parallel having opposite sense, the pairs x, y±π∈
T R2 do not carry a region Ω ⊂ R2. This is due to the existence of parallel tangents, see [3].

5. Topological transitions

perturbation on the ﬁnal direction (while ﬁxing the ﬁnal position) the resultant endpoints

homotopic to paths of arbitrary length due to the existence of parallel tangents, see Corollary

isotopy classes.

Homotopy classes and deformations of Dubins paths
We would like to motivate a theory analyzing algorithmic aspects of deformations of piecewise
bounded curvature paths of constant curvature. Many standard questions in computational
geometry can be adapted for this class of paths.
In [6] we deﬁned operations on bounded curvature paths being a ﬁnite number of concatena-
tions of line segments and arcs of unit radius circles, the so-called cs paths. The line segments
and arcs of circles are called components. The number of components is called the complexity
of the path 3. Also in [6], we proved that a cs path can be constructed arbitrarily close to
any given bounded curvature path. It is of interest to study the computational complexity of
deforming cs paths.
It is not hard to see that for any given x,y∈ T R2 the space Γ( x,y) has a ﬁnite number of
Dubins paths. In Example 9.2 we index Dubins paths according to their length. The length
minimizer in Γ( x,y) is denoted by γ0.

global minima, existence of local maxima, and deformations of cs paths. In Fig. 14 we consider
seven illustrations, and in Example 9.2 we consider seven items. We associate illustrations and
